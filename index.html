Conceptual Foundation First

Before any procedure, students must understand: a ÷ b asks "how many groups of b fit into a?" The fraction tile model makes this visible — line up divisor tiles under the dividend and count how many fit. Work through the three examples below. Press Show Groups on each to see the answer revealed.

📖 How to Read the Tile Model
1/2
Dividend row
All bars = same width (1 whole). Colored tiles show your fraction. Each tile is labeled with its unit fraction.
1 group row
Shows what one divisor-group looks like. Different tile widths = different fraction sizes.
Groups bar
Each color = one group of the divisor placed consecutively. Dashed green line = end of dividend.
EXAMPLE 1 · Exact Groups
½ ÷ ⅙

🍕 You have ½ of a pizza. Each slice is ⅙ of the whole pizza. How many slices do you have?

EXAMPLE 2 · Same Denominator
¾ ÷ ¼

🧁 You have ¾ cup of sugar. Each batch of cookies needs ¼ cup. How many batches can you make?

EXAMPLE 3 · Partial Group
⅔ ÷ ½

🚶 Rosa's school is ⅔ of a mile away. She walks in segments of ½ mile before resting. How many full segments does she walk — and how far through the last segment does she get?

Note: The answer 1⅓ means she completes 1 full half-mile segment and then walks ⅓ of a second segment (i.e., ⅓ × ½ = ⅙ of a mile further). Distance is continuous — a partial segment makes sense here!

💡 The Big Idea
When the divisor is smaller than the dividend
More than one group fits → quotient > 1
Example: ½ ÷ ⅙ = 3
When the divisor is larger than the dividend
Less than one group fits → quotient < 1
Example: ¼ ÷ ½ = ½
⚠️ Context matters for partial groups! A fractional quotient only makes real-world sense when the quantity is continuous (distance, liquid, time, fabric length). Avoid contexts like "how many bows can you make?" — you can't make ⅓ of a bow! Choose contexts where being partway through a group is meaningful.
